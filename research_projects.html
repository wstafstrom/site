Research Projects

Monitoring Mycotoxins in Low-Resource Settings

Chronic and acute mycotoxin exposure in humans mostly occurs in areas that lack the capacity to maintain resource-intensive testing and regulatory systems. Mycotoxin monitoring and mitigation approaches in these areas should incorporate cost-effective mycotoxin detection, regional cooperation, community feedback, and context-appropriate responses. For more detail on these issues refer to my book chapter, Mycotoxin Surveillance for Low-resource Settings.

Genetic Relationships Among Yield-Component and Resistance Loci

A high-yielding maize variety is a testament to both the effectiveness of modern maize breeding and to the diversity of maize that has been cultivated and improved upon over thousands of years. Are there unintended consequences when the main breeding target is to increase yield? I am using a near-isogenic line population with diverse founder lines to identify introgressions that control maize yield-components (ear, cob, and kernel traits), and then examining those introgressions under Fusarium ear rot pressure to see whether there is an inherent tradeoff between yield and ear diseases.

Modelling Mycotoxins Across Landscapes

In Tanzanian smallholder farming systems, contamination of maize by aflatoxins and fumonisins is common in hot and dry environments. Where, when, and to what extent does this contamination occur? For consumers of maize in these systems, an array of surveillable risk factors exist along the maize value chain; pre-harvest environmental conditions (e.g. drought, poor soil health), post-harvest storage conditions (e.g. high moisture), and processing decisions (e.g. dehulling) interact and affect the level of mycotoxin contamination in maize. I performed a survey of local grain mills in Kongwa district, Tanzania and assayed georeferenced maize aflatoxins and fumonisins. Statistical modeling of aflatoxin and fumonisin concentrations was enabled by a combination of 1) remote sensing information of pre-harvest risk factors, 2) surveyed risk factors from farmers, and 3) NIR scans of the maize.

Low-Tech Sorting of Mycotoxin-Contaminated Maize Grain

Mycotoxins heterogeneously contaminate maize ears, and a variety of sorting methods can effectively reduce contamination by removing highly toxic grains. A principal challenge of sorting in low-resource settings is balancing the inherent tradeoff between food security (retaining as much safe grain as possible) and food safety (removing as much toxic grain as possible). The DropSort is a low-cost density-based sorting device that tries to achieve this balance. You can read more about the DropSort's affect on fumonisin contamination in my publication, Validation and Application of a Low-Cost Sorting Device for Fumonisin Reduction in Maize
